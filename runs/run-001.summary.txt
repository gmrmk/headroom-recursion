## 2. ESTABLISHED

1. **[KNOWN] Relativization barrier.** There exist oracles A, B with P^A = NP^A and P^B ≠ NP^B (Baker, Gill, Solovay (1975)). Any technique preserved under relativization cannot resolve P vs NP.

2. **[KNOWN] Algebrization barrier.** Aaronson and Wigderson (2008) exhibit algebrizing analogues of the BGS oracles, so arithmetization-based techniques (strictly stronger than relativizing ones) cannot by themselves separate P from NP.

3. **[KNOWN] Karp–Lipton theorem.** If NP ⊆ P/poly then PH collapses to Σ2^p (Karp and Lipton (1980)). A guessed poly-size SAT-circuit is universally checkable via SAT's downward self-reducibility, collapsing any Π2^p statement to two alternations.

4. **[KNOWN] Kannan's fixed-polynomial circuit lower bound.** For every constant k, Σ2^p ∩ Π2^p ⊄ SIZE(n^k) (Kannan (1982)). Cited for statement only; the Karp–Lipton case split is not reconstructed here.

5. **[KNOWN] Natural proofs barrier.** A property of Boolean functions (a subset of {0,1}^N, N = 2^n, tested on truth tables) is *natural* if it is (a) **constructive** — membership decidable in poly(N) = 2^{O(n)} time from the truth table; (b) **large** — satisfied by a ≥ 1/poly(N) fraction of n-input functions; (c) **useful** — no P/poly family satisfies it for all but finitely many n. Razborov and Rudich (1997): if strong (exponentially hard) pseudorandom function generators exist, then no natural property is useful against P/poly. *This is a meta-theorem about the limits of lower-bound methods; usefulness (5c) asserts that the property's members are hard for P/poly, and places no specific language into the property.*

6. **[KNOWN] MCSP definition and NP-membership.** Fix a finite complete basis. MCSP[s] takes a truth table T ∈ {0,1}^N (N = 2^n, encoding f : {0,1}^n → {0,1}) and threshold s = s(n) in binary, and asks whether CC(f) ≤ s. **Argument (complete):** a certificate names a circuit C with ≤ s gates — each gate O(log(s+n)) bits for its ≤ 2 input wires among ≤ s+n available, plus O(1) for type — so |cert| = O(s·log(s+n)); the verifier evaluates C on all N inputs in O(N·s) time. Both are poly(N) when s ≤ poly(N). Hence MCSP ∈ NP (Kabanets and Cai (2000), building on Trakhtenbrot–Yablonski circuit minimization).

7. **[KNOWN] MCSP's NP-hardness status is open.** Whether MCSP is NP-hard under poly-time many-one reductions is an explicit long-standing open problem (Kabanets and Cai (2000)); standard local/gadget reductions have not been made to work against it.

8. **[KNOWN] Circuit counting; largeness of the high-complexity property.** Over a fixed finite basis the number of circuits on n inputs with ≤ s gates (s ≥ n) is at most s^{O(s)} = 2^{O(s log s)} (each gate: O(log s) wire bits + O(1) type bits). Mapping each function of circuit complexity ≤ s to one witnessing circuit is injective, so at most 2^{O(s log s)} functions have CC ≤ s (Arora and Barak (2009), Ch. 6). Two instances: **(i)** s = n^k gives ≤ 2^{O(n^k log n)} functions of CC ≤ n^k, a fraction → 0 of the 2^{2^n} functions, so "CC > n^k" has density → 1 (large). **(ii)** s = 2^{εn} (ε < 1) gives ≤ 2^{O(εn·2^{εn})} = 2^{o(2^n)} functions of CC ≤ 2^{εn}, so "CC > 2^{εn}" also has density → 1 (large). Both far exceed RR's 1/poly(N) largeness threshold.

9. **[KNOWN, folklore] Threshold dichotomy for usefulness against P/poly.** For a threshold function s(n), the property H_s = {f : CC(f) > s(n)} is useful against P/poly **if and only if** s(n) is superpolynomial (s = n^{ω(1)}).
   **Argument (complete).**
   *(⇐)* If s = n^{ω(1)}, then any P/poly family {g_n} has CC(g_n) ≤ n^c for a fixed c and all large n, so CC(g_n) < s(n) for all but finitely many n; hence {g_n} ∉ H_s for all but finitely many n. Useful.
   *(⇒, contrapositive)* Suppose s(n) ≤ n^k for a fixed k and infinitely many n. Put m = ⌈(k+2)·log₂ n⌉. By the Shannon–Lupanov counting bound, some function g on m bits has CC(g) = Θ(2^m/m). Let f_n : {0,1}^n → {0,1} depend only on the first m input bits and compute g there. Then CC(f_n) = Θ(2^m/m) = Θ(n^{k+2}/log n) > n^k ≥ s(n) for all large n in that infinite set, while CC(f_n) ≤ 2^m = O(n^{k+2}) = poly(n), so {f_n} ∈ P/poly. Thus a P/poly family lies in H_s infinitely often: not useful.
   **Consequence.** The fixed-degree route is closed negatively: resolving MCSP[n^k] ∈ P? cannot yield a natural proof against P/poly, because the associated property is not even useful. Only a superpolynomial threshold survives.

10. **[KNOWN] The superpolynomial-threshold property is large and useful against all P/poly.** Take s = 2^{εn} (0 < ε < 1) and the total set Π = {f : CC(f) > 2^{εn}} = complement of MCSP[2^{εn}]. By item 9(⇐), Π is **useful against all P/poly** (any P/poly family has size ≤ n^{O(1)} < 2^{εn} for large n). By item 8(ii), Π is **large**. Hence Π satisfies RR largeness and usefulness unconditionally; the **only** missing natural-proof ingredient is **constructivity** — whether membership in Π is decidable in poly(N) time from the truth table, equivalently whether MCSP[2^{εn}] ∈ DTIME(poly(N)). This is the standard "high-complexity property as a natural property" observation implicit in Razborov and Rudich (1997) and around Kabanets and Cai (2000). *Note: "CC(f) > 2^{εn} > poly" is literally "f ∉ P/poly," so item 9(⇐) here restates that definition; the content of item 10 is entirely in the constructivity question, i.e. it is a renaming of MCSP[2^{εn}] ∈ P?.*

11. **[KNOWN] External consequences of MCSP[2^{εn}] ∈ P (lateral to the grand target).** If MCSP[2^{εn}] ∈ P then Π (item 10) is a natural property useful against P/poly, and therefore:
    **(a)** by Razborov and Rudich (1997) read contrapositively, strong (exponentially hard) pseudorandom function generators do **not** exist;
    **(b)** by Carmosino, Impagliazzo, Kabanets, Kolokolova (2016), a natural property useful against P/poly yields a **quasi-polynomial-time learning algorithm** for P/poly under the uniform distribution with membership queries.
    These are published implications (a cryptographic non-existence result and a learning algorithm). **Neither is a circuit lower bound for any NP language**, and both are directions the field widely believes false; they are payoffs orthogonal to separating P from NP (see item 12).

12. **[NEW-framing of KNOWN facts] Neither branch of the constructivity question separates P from NP.**
    **Argument (complete).** *Positive branch.* Suppose MCSP[2^{εn}] ∈ P, so Π is natural and useful against P/poly. Usefulness (item 5c) asserts only that *every function in Π* is hard for P/poly; it says nothing about whether any particular NP language (e.g. SAT's characteristic function at each length) belongs to Π. Membership of SAT in Π would *itself be* the statement NP ⊄ P/poly and is not implied by Π being useful. Thus the positive branch yields items 11(a),(b) and a natural distinguisher between hard and easy truth tables, but **no** lower bound for NP. *Negative branch.* MCSP[2^{εn}] ∉ P (or hardness evidence) isolates constructivity as the obstruction to naturalizing Π but proves no separation. **Conclusion:** the constructivity question has grand-target altitude 0 in both branches. This is not a defect to be repaired but a structural fact: the natural-proofs framework is a barrier meta-theorem, and no barrier-side question about it can, by itself, generate an NP lower bound. Recorded here to prevent any "usefulness ⇒ separation" overclaim.

## 3. FRONTIER

**Next target (precise).** Decide **MCSP[2^{εn}] ∈ DTIME(poly(N))?** (N = 2^n) — equivalently, whether Π = {f : CC(f) > 2^{εn}} is RR-*constructive*. By item 9's dichotomy the same question at any superpolynomial threshold is the relevant one; 2^{εn} is the canonical, poly(N)-encodable representative.

**(a) What it would and would not do (no "suffices" claim).** A positive answer completes Π to a *natural property useful against P/poly* and triggers item 11 (no strong PRFs; a P/poly learner under uniform + queries). By item 12 this is a **barrier-plus-payoff outcome, not a separation**: it proves no NP language hard, and is a direction believed false. A negative answer isolates constructivity as the exact obstruction, again with no separation. **This target does not, in either branch, advance the grand target P ≠ NP**; it advances the attack line's stated (lateral) goal of mapping the natural-proofs barrier around P/poly. I state this explicitly rather than claim a bridge that does not exist.

**(b) Obstacle.** MCSP[2^{εn}]'s relationship to P is as hard as MCSP's generally (items 6–7). The **downward self-reducibility** that powers Karp–Lipton (item 3) — building a small circuit for f incrementally from small circuits for restrictions of f — is **not known to hold for MCSP or its threshold variants**. Without it, neither a poly(N)-time decision procedure nor a hardness proof is available, which is exactly why the constructivity question is open.

**(c) Candidate strategy — and why it is expected-negative.** One could seek a **self-reducibility lemma**: *a poly(N)-time procedure that, given the truth table of f on n bits together with correct MCSP[2^{εn′}] answers on all restrictions of f to n′ < n bits, decides MCSP[2^{εn}] on f.* Such a lemma would give a bottom-up dynamic program over restriction-arity (poly(N) time, hence constructivity). **However**, by item 11(a) a positive resolution implies strong PRFs do not exist — a breakthrough the field expects to be **false** — so this strategy targets a believed-false statement and should be treated as expected-negative, not as a promising path to a win. The more honest live directions are (i) accumulating *hardness* evidence for MCSP[2^{εn}] (the negative branch), or (ii) **pivoting the attack line** entirely, since item 12 shows neither branch reaches the grand target. The lemma is open and not proved here.
